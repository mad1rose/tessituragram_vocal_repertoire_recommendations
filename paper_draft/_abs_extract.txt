**Motivation.** Vocal health depends on choosing repertoire that fits the singer. Existing software can summarize tessitura for a score the user already has open; it does not typically **search a library** for other pieces whose tessitura matches stated needs. **Tessitura** here means how much singing time falls on each pitch, weighted by note length.

**Method.** We present a **content-based** ranker: each song is represented as a **tessituragram** (duration-weighted pitch histogram) from a machine-readable score. The user specifies vocal range, favorite pitches, and pitches to avoid. We keep only songs that fit inside the range, build an ideal pitch profile from the user’s lists, and rank by cosine similarity to that profile, minus a term that down-weights time spent on avoided pitches.

**Evaluation (offline).** We use **synthetic self-retrieval**, a standard way to test the system when there are no human ratings. For each trial we pick one vocal line in the library, build the user profile **from that line’s own tessituragram** (same written range; favorites = the pitches with the most total duration, avoids = the least), and ask whether the ranker places **that same line** near the top among **other** songs that survive the range filter. High recovery means the implementation matches its design; it does **not** by itself show how real singers would behave.

**Studies.** We use two **different** designs on the same broad art-song material (Method)—not two independent experiments: (1) 101 songs, one line per piece; (2) 1,655 lines from 1,419 pieces, with larger pools of candidates per query. A **null** baseline range-filters, then orders candidates uniformly at random. Against it, the full model reaches HR@1 **76%** vs **6%** (design 1) and **55%** vs **2%** (design 2), and HR@5 **86%** vs **7%** (design 2). Small edits to preferences change rankings only modestly (Kendall's τ ≈ **0.84–0.85**). A **cosine-only** variant (drop the avoid term) is not clearly better or worse than the full model on primary metrics in the large-library design; in the small-library sample, cosine-only scores higher on top-1 hit rate. Overall, the evidence supports **internal consistency** of the implementation; real users and other repertoires are future work.
